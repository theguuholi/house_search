# ADR-001: Broker-Controlled Evidence-Backed Shortlists

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-07-15

## Context

HouseSearch must reduce property-research time without presenting AI output as
professional judgment. Approved sources may contain incomplete, stale, or
duplicated listings, and a confirmed request may produce fewer than three exact
matches. The product must preserve the broker's control while still helping
when the inventory is sparse.

## Decision

HouseSearch produces a ranked shortlist from verified listing data. The normal
shortlist contains the three highest-ranked distinct properties that meet all
hard requirements; it does not force diversity by source, location, or
trade-off profile. Each option states why it matches, any disclosed compromise,
its source link, and its latest verification time.

When fewer than three exact matches exist, HouseSearch shows the exact matches
and keeps near-matches in a separate section. A near-match can enter the
shortlist only after the broker explicitly approves its stated compromise. The
broker must approve the final shortlist before generating client-ready content.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Automatically fill all three positions with near-matches

- **Description**: Always return three options, using the closest alternatives
  when exact inventory is insufficient.
- **Pros**: Preserves a visually complete Top 3 and minimizes interaction.
- **Cons**: Can silently weaken requirements and make unsuitable properties
  appear endorsed.
- **Why rejected**: It conflicts with the product's trust and broker-control
  principles.

### Alternative 2: Return exact matches only

- **Description**: Return fewer than three properties whenever exact inventory
  is insufficient and omit near-matches.
- **Pros**: Strictly preserves confirmed requirements.
- **Cons**: Hides potentially useful alternatives and gives the broker no
  assisted path forward.
- **Why rejected**: Separate, consent-based near-matches provide more value
  without weakening requirements automatically.

### Alternative 3: Force three different trade-off profiles

- **Description**: Intentionally diversify the shortlist even when a lower
  scoring property must replace a stronger match.
- **Pros**: Exposes the client to a wider variety of choices.
- **Cons**: Makes the ranking less faithful to the confirmed request.
- **Why rejected**: Deduplication is required, but artificial diversity is not
  part of the MVP promise.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Brokers remain accountable for every client-facing recommendation.
- Hard requirements are never relaxed silently.
- Sparse inventory still yields useful, transparent alternatives.
- Every recommendation remains auditable through its evidence and source.

### Negative

- Some cases finish with fewer than three broker-approved options.
- Approving near-matches adds an interaction before a shortlist is complete.
- Client-ready content cannot be generated fully autonomously.

### Risks

- Brokers may approve compromises without reading them; the product must make
  each failed hard requirement prominent before approval.
- Similar listings may survive deduplication; uncertain duplicates must be
  disclosed rather than silently merged.

## Implementation Notes

The TechSpec must define the exact ranking, evidence, deduplication, and approval
mechanisms while preserving these observable rules.
