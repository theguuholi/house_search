# ADR-002: Confirmed Criteria and Supported Pilot Inventory

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-07-15

## Context

Client requests mix objective constraints with subjective preferences. Treating
every phrase as equally strict can eliminate useful inventory, while inferring
softness without confirmation can violate the client's actual requirements.
The pilot also needs an explicit geographic and property boundary.

## Decision

The assistant proposes structured criteria and classifies each one as a hard
requirement or preference. The broker reviews, edits, and confirms the complete
classification before a search case opens.

The MVP supports price, city, neighborhood, property type, bedrooms, bathrooms,
parking spaces, floor area, and free-form preferences that can be supported by
listing evidence. It does not interpret subjective neighborhood judgments such
as "safe" or "family-friendly" as search criteria; it asks the broker to
replace them with objective, buyer-confirmed requirements.

The pilot covers residential property sales in Americana, Santa Bárbara
d'Oeste, and Nova Odessa. Supported property types are houses, townhouses,
condominium houses, and apartments.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Fixed hard and soft criteria

- **Description**: Always treat price, property type, and city as hard and all
  other criteria as preferences.
- **Pros**: Simple and consistent.
- **Cons**: Cannot represent a buyer for whom bedrooms, parking, or neighborhood
  is non-negotiable.
- **Why rejected**: The broker must control the meaning of the client's request.

### Alternative 2: Require manual classification without defaults

- **Description**: Make the broker label every criterion from scratch.
- **Pros**: Maximizes explicit control.
- **Cons**: Adds repetitive work and weakens the conversational value.
- **Why rejected**: Proposed classifications with confirmation provide the same
  control with less effort.

### Alternative 3: Include enriched neighborhood judgments

- **Description**: Support safety, family suitability, school quality, crime,
  demographics, and commute data in the MVP.
- **Pros**: Captures more natural buyer language.
- **Cons**: Introduces inconsistent data, steering risk, and claims that listing
  evidence cannot reliably support.
- **Why rejected**: Objective criteria and evidence-backed preferences are the
  safer, verifiable pilot boundary.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Brokers see and control the exact interpretation before usage is consumed.
- Ranking can distinguish eligibility from preference strength.
- The pilot has a precise inventory and geographic boundary.
- Unsupported subjective requests fail safely instead of producing invented
  neighborhood claims.

### Negative

- Criteria confirmation requires an explicit review step.
- Some natural-language requests require clarification or reformulation.
- Land, rural property, and new-development-specific workflows remain excluded.

### Risks

- The assistant may propose the wrong classification; confirmation must make
  every hard requirement and preference visible and editable.
- Free-form preferences may lack evidence; unsupported preferences must appear
  as unknown, never as satisfied.

## Implementation Notes

The TechSpec must define validated representations for supported criteria and
the user-visible handling of unsupported or ambiguous language.
